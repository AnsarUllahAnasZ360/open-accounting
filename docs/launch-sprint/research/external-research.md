## AI CFO / automated financial advisory for SMBs: proactive, ledger-grounded advice and recurring digest products. The competitive frontier (Digits, Puzzle, Fathom, Ramp Intelligence, Bench, virtual-CFO firms) has converged on a recognizable pattern: compute a small set of operator metrics from books that are kept current continuously (runway, burn, margin, AR/AP aging, cash position), surface variances "as they occur instead of after close," and package the rest as a recurring digest plus always-on anomaly flags. OpenBooks' edge: it already owns an immutable double-entry ledger, so every advisory number can be derived from journal lines (auditable, drill-down-able) rather than ad-hoc category totals — which is exactly the "trust" gap reviewers flag in Digits' beautiful-but-opaque dashboards. The strategic move is to ship an "Advisor" layer that is (1) deterministic where it can be (runway = cash / trailing burn, tax set-aside = % of net income, concentration = top-customer share), (2) AI-narrated on top of those deterministic numbers, and (3) framed as informational guidance, never regulated financial/tax/investment advice — because courts and regulators are increasingly skeptical of thin "not advice" disclaimers when the UI delivers what functions as authoritative guidance.

**Principles**

- Keep the books continuously current, then advise off them. Every competitor's headline claim is that they surface burn/runway/variances 'as they occur' or '3 weeks faster than month-end close' (Puzzle), 'not 45 days ago' (Digits). The advisory product is only as good as the freshness of the underlying ledger — OpenBooks' continuous Plaid/Stripe pipeline is the prerequisite, not the feature.
- Advice must be deterministic-first, AI-narrated-second. The load-bearing numbers (runway, burn, tax reserve, concentration %, aging) are arithmetic on journal lines and should be computed by code, not generated by the model. Use the LLM only to explain, prioritize, and phrase — never to invent the figure. This is the exact trust gap reviewers hit with Digits: 'brilliant dashboards' but users can't fully verify how a number was derived.
- Make every insight drill-down to source. Digits' praised behavior is 'click a spike in expenses and drill down to see exactly what caused it.' Because OpenBooks numbers come from immutable journal lines, every advisory claim can link to the exact transactions/entries behind it. Auditability is the differentiator, not an afterthought.
- Two delivery modes: always-on flags + a recurring digest. The pattern across Ramp (real-time anomaly/duplicate flagging), Puzzle (variances flagged as they occur), and virtual-CFO firms (regular monthly reports/forecasts) is a split between (a) instant alerts for things that need action now and (b) a calm weekly/monthly summary that builds the operator's mental model over time.
- Flag anomalies against a learned baseline, not fixed thresholds. Ramp 'learns what normal spending looks like for a business, then flags transactions that don't fit the pattern.' Per-vendor / per-category baselines (trailing average + variance band) beat global rules and produce far fewer false positives.
- Frame as informational guidance, not regulated advice — and back it with honest numbers, not just a disclaimer. Courts are 'skeptical of this is not financial advice disclaimers when the interface is designed to deliver what functions as authoritative financial guidance.' Responsible phrasing names limits explicitly: 'for guidance purposes only; does not constitute financial, tax, or investment advice; does not take your personal circumstances into account.' The disclaimer is necessary but the real protection is grounding every claim in the user's own ledger data and avoiding directives ('you should borrow', 'invest in').
- Benchmarking needs a real comparison set or it's noise. Industry benchmarking compares revenue, gross/net margin, COGS, cash flow against peers; the value comes from a credible dataset (RMA, IBISWorld, trade-association studies). Until OpenBooks has a peer corpus, prefer self-benchmarking (this month vs trailing 3/6/12mo, vs same month last year) which is honest and immediately useful.
- Phrase money in plain English at the owner layer, hide the double-entry. Consistent with OpenBooks' North Star: the advisor speaks like a CFO ('You have about 7 months of runway at your current spend'), while the ledger stays the hidden system of record.

**Apply to OpenBooks**

- Advisor metrics engine (deterministic, ledger-derived): a single module that computes from journal lines — Cash position (sum of cash/bank account balances), Net burn (trailing-30/90-day net cash outflow), Runway (cash / average monthly burn), Gross & net margin, MoM revenue trend, AR aging buckets + DSO, AP aging + upcoming bills due. All as integer minor units + currency. Every output carries the entry IDs it was derived from so the UI can drill down.
- Runway & burn card with scenarios: show 'X months of runway at current burn,' plus simple what-if sliders ('if revenue stays flat,' 'if you cut $Y/mo,' 'if you hire one person at $Z') that recompute runway. This is the Fathom 'driver-based / scenario modelling' idea reduced to 2-3 levers a solo owner actually controls — not a full three-way model.
- Tax set-aside meter: continuously track a recommended reserve = configurable % (default ~25-30% of net income, surfaced as a range) of profit since the last estimated-tax date, with a 'you have / you should have set aside' gauge and quarterly due-date reminders. Mirrors Bench/Found/Keeper calculators but live off the actual ledger instead of a one-off form. Explicitly labeled an estimate, not tax advice; never auto-moves money.
- Anomaly & duplicate detection inbox: per-vendor/per-category trailing baseline; flag (a) a charge materially above a vendor's normal amount, (b) likely duplicate (same vendor + amount + near date), (c) a new recurring vendor, (d) a subscription that's been charging with no offsetting activity ('unused subscription'). These become Inbox items with one-click confirm/dismiss — fits OpenBooks' 'uncertain items go to Inbox' loop. Mirrors Ramp's duplicate/unused-subscription and Puzzle's variance flags.
- Expense-creep & revenue-trend alerts: detect categories trending up over 3 months ('Software spend up 38% over 3 months, driven by 2 new vendors') and revenue dips ('Revenue down 2 months running; top customer billed $0 this month'). Each links to the contributing transactions.
- Customer / vendor concentration insight: compute top-customer share of trailing revenue and flag concentration risk ('Acme is 61% of last-quarter revenue'); same for vendor dependency on the spend side. Pure ledger arithmetic.
- Weekly digest (email via existing Plunk integration) + monthly review: a short, scannable summary — cash & runway, money in vs out, biggest movements, anything in the Inbox needing attention, tax-reserve status, one or two AI-written 'what changed and why it matters' notes. Cadence echoes virtual-CFO monthly reporting and Puzzle/Digits 'always know where you stand.' Each line deep-links into the app.
- AI narration grounded in the metrics object: feed the deterministic metrics + recent flags to the model to produce the digest prose and the 'Ask AI' answers, with a hard rule that it may only reference figures present in the supplied ledger-derived payload (no fabricated numbers), and must attach source drill-downs. Reuse the existing Ask AI / AI Elements widget.
- Insight provenance + responsible-framing layer: every advisory card shows 'based on your books through {date}' and an info affordance with the standard non-advice language; the advisor never issues directives to take on debt, invest, or make tax filings — it surfaces facts and options. Store a generated_at and source_entries trail for auditability.
- Self-benchmarking now, peer-benchmarking later: ship trailing-period and year-over-year comparisons immediately (honest, no external data needed); design the metric schema so an industry-peer comparison can slot in later if/when a dataset exists, without reworking the cards.

**Pitfalls**

- Letting the LLM compute or 'tweak' the load-bearing numbers. Runway, burn, tax reserve, and concentration must be code-computed from journal lines; the model only narrates. Anything else risks a confidently wrong figure — the exact failure mode the Empower/UniSG sources warn about ('LLMs wrap advice in confident, friendly language').
- Thin disclaimer theater. A bare 'not financial advice' line offers weak protection when the UI 'delivers what functions as authoritative financial guidance.' Avoid imperative/directive language ('you should take a loan / invest / claim this deduction'); present facts and options, name personal-circumstances limits, and keep tax outputs labeled estimates.
- Alarm-styling ordinary spend. Per OpenBooks design rules, money-out is neutral, not alarm red. Anomaly flags should read as 'worth a look,' not panic — and false positives must be cheap to dismiss or owners will mute the whole feature.
- Fixed-threshold anomaly rules. Global thresholds spam users; learn per-vendor/per-category baselines (Ramp's approach). A $400 charge is normal for one vendor and alarming for another.
- Fake or unsourced benchmarking. Claiming industry comparison without a credible peer dataset (RMA/IBISWorld/trade studies) is misleading. Start with honest self-benchmarking (trailing periods, YoY) rather than inventing a 'vs industry' percentile.
- Stale advice. Runway/burn computed off books that haven't synced is worse than no number. Gate the digest/cards on data freshness and always stamp 'based on your books through {date}.'
- Digest fatigue / noise. If the weekly email isn't genuinely scannable and prioritized (top 1-3 things that changed), it gets filtered. Borrow the virtual-CFO 'one page that matters' discipline, not a data dump.
- Floats and currency drift in metric math. Burn, runway, and reserve calculations must stay in integer minor units + currency code per OpenBooks rules; rounding/averaging across months is a classic place floats sneak in.
- Auto-moving money for tax set-aside. Compute and recommend the reserve; do not initiate transfers — that crosses from informational guidance toward regulated/agency behavior and breaks the 'AI proposes, owner decides' contract.

## Best-practice guided onboarding and activation for AI bookkeeping: a progressive setup wizard that reaches a real "your books are set up" value moment fast (target under 15-20 min, ideally first activation within 24h), built on an AI-proposes/you-approve spine — connect bank -> AI drafts the chart of accounts/categories/rules -> owner approves -> first report renders. Skippable steps, endowed-progress checklists, and a sample/demo workspace cover the cold-start so the empty state never feels empty. This mirrors how Digits ("95% configured with no manual input"), Puzzle (98% auto-categorization that learns from corrections), QuickBooks (industry-driven auto-generated chart of accounts) and Xero (bank feed in the first 30-60 min) win activation, while staying inside OpenBooks' "AI proposes, the ledger posts" rule.

**Principles**

- Define ONE observable activation event and design backward from it, not from checklist completion. Userpilot: define it narrowly ('published their first flow') not as a proxy like 'logged in 3x'. For OpenBooks the activation event should be 'bank connected AND first batch of AI-categorized transactions approved AND first P&L/cash report rendered' — not 'finished the wizard'.
- Compress time-to-value hard. Self-serve products should hit first value inside ~15 minutes; products over ~30 min TTFV see roughly 3x higher abandonment, and ~25% of users abandon after a single use if they don't see value. Target activation within the first session and definitely within 24h.
- Use the AI-proposes / human-approves spine as the core flow, not a feature bolt-on. Industry 'Level 2' pattern: AI suggests categorization + chart of accounts, human decides; anything the model is <~95% confident on goes to a Review Queue rather than auto-posting. This is exactly OpenBooks' 'AI proposes, the ledger posts' with thresholds suggest=never-autopost / balanced=0.90 / autopilot=0.75.
- Never show a blank empty state — seed direction or sample data. Empty states must give the next best action; AI-native tools (Notion's auto-generated sample planner, Airtable Omni) generate starter content to beat 'empty canvas paralysis'. Provide a sandbox/demo workspace with sample transactions so the owner experiences the categorize->approve->report loop before their real bank data finishes syncing in the background.
- Run setup asynchronously and let the user act immediately. Best practice: do real setup (bank sync, transaction import, AI classification) in the background while the user explores sample data or completes quick wins; don't block the value moment on a slow API. Bank feeds can lag days, so design the wizard to proceed and backfill.
- Personalize with a max ~2-question welcome microsurvey, then use the answers to auto-configure. Calendly/Figma/Coursera ask role/goal upfront; QuickBooks asks 'what does your business do' + 'what tax form you file' and auto-populates a predefined, industry-specific chart of accounts. Use business type + entity to pick the AI's starting chart of accounts and category set.
- Use an endowed-progress checklist of 3-5 activation-mapped items with a pre-checked first step. Pre-checking even one item (Sked Social) drove a 3x conversion lift; progress bars/checklists lift completion ~20-30% (Zeigarnik effect). Items must map to the owner's goal ('Connect your bank') not your feature list ('Explore integrations').
- Make every step skippable and separate required setup from optional learning. Calendly splits required upfront questions from optional side-panel videos. Required for OpenBooks: connect a money source + approve the AI's first categorizations. Everything else (logo, invoice branding, payroll, extra accounts) should be deferrable with a persistent checklist to return to.
- Teach by doing inside a real (or realistic) workspace, not a passive tour. Interactive walkthroughs that require the user to complete the action outperform 'Next'-button tours (Attention Insight +10% activation). The owner should approve a real AI-proposed category as the teaching moment, learning the approve/correct loop on live stakes.
- Frame the system in plain English and keep the double-entry ledger hidden. OpenBooks' north star is plain-English owner experience over a hidden double-entry ledger; the wizard should say 'we set up your books' / 'we'll sort your transactions', never expose debits/credits or COA jargon during onboarding (QBO/Xero leak accounting terms here — an opportunity to differentiate).
- Close the learning loop and show it. AI that 'learns from your corrections, not static rules' (Puzzle) is the durable hook; surface that every correction trains the rules/memory so the owner sees their approvals reducing future work, reinforcing the activation habit.

**Apply to OpenBooks**

- Build a linear-but-skippable onboarding wizard: (1) 2-question welcome microsurvey (business type + entity/tax form) -> (2) AI drafts a plain-English chart of accounts + category set from those answers -> (3) connect a money source (Plaid sandbox / Stripe test / CSV-OFX import) -> (4) AI classifies the first imported batch -> (5) owner reviews & approves a handful of proposed categorizations -> (6) first P&L + cash snapshot renders as the 'your books are set up' moment. Each step has Skip and a Back, and writes progress to a persistent activation checklist.
- Add an AI-proposed Chart of Accounts approval screen: pre-fill an industry-appropriate COA/category list (driven by the microsurvey), show it in plain English with the hidden double-entry mapping behind it, and let the owner approve-all, rename, or remove in one pass — never make them build a COA from scratch. Mirror QuickBooks' auto-populated industry chart but without exposing accounting jargon.
- Wire the activation event explicitly in analytics/state: 'firstSourceConnected', 'firstBatchClassified', 'firstApproval', 'firstReportViewed'. Treat the combination (source connected + >=1 approval + report rendered) as Activated, and instrument median time-to-activation, not wizard-completion rate. (PostHog is already in this workspace for funnel tracking.)
- Make the Inbox the human-in-the-loop Review Queue surfaced during onboarding: anything below the active confidence threshold (0.90 balanced / 0.75 autopilot, suggest=never) lands in the Inbox with a one-tap approve/correct. Onboarding should route the owner through approving their first 3-5 Inbox items as a guided quick win.
- Ship a sample/demo workspace and rich empty states: every page (Income/Expenses/Bills/Reports/Inbox) shows a next-best-action empty state, and the owner can explore a seeded sample company (sample transactions already AI-categorized) so they see the approve->report loop while Plaid/Stripe sync runs in the background. Drop the existing demo-seed behind an explicit 'Try with sample data' affordance.
- Run connection + import + classification asynchronously with visible status (e.g. 'Importing your transactions… you can keep setting up'), and let the owner proceed to approve sample/early items rather than blocking on the full sync. Backfill real transactions into the same review flow when the feed lands.
- Add an endowed-progress activation checklist to the dashboard (3-5 items: Connect your bank [pre-checked once a source is added], Approve your first transactions, See your first report, Add your business details, Invite your accountant), with a progress bar and a dismiss/explore-on-my-own exit. Persist it post-onboarding so partial setups can be resumed.
- Surface the learning loop in the UI: after each correction show a quiet confirmation that 'we'll remember this' and, on the dashboard, a small 'X% of your transactions sorted automatically' metric that climbs as approvals accumulate — making OpenBooks' rules/memory engine visible the way Puzzle/Digits market their auto-categorization rate.
- Keep onboarding copy plain-English and quiet per the design system (white ledger surfaces, Geist, one brand green, no purple AI styling/gradients/emoji): label the AI moment as 'We set up your books' / 'We sorted these for you — does this look right?' rather than 'AI categorization engine', and present money-in green / expenses neutral.

**Pitfalls**

- Measuring wizard/checklist completion instead of a real activation event. A user can finish every step and still churn; PLG checklist completion is only ~19% (vs ~22% sales-led). Instrument 'connected + approved + saw a report', not 'finished onboarding'.
- Blocking the value moment on slow bank feeds. Bank/Plaid feeds and Stripe webhooks can lag (Xero advises starting feeds ~1 week early); if the wizard waits for a full sync the owner stalls at an empty screen. Run sync async and let them act on sample/early data.
- Showing a blank empty state with no next action, or dumping misleading dummy data the owner mistakes for real books. Empty states must point to the next best action; sample data must be clearly labeled as sample.
- Over-long, non-skippable wizards. Complex mandatory setup, email verification before any access, and forced profile completion are classic TTV killers; >30 min TTFV ~3x abandonment. Keep required steps to connect-source + approve-first-batch; defer the rest.
- Exposing double-entry / accounting jargon (debits, credits, build-your-own COA) during onboarding. OpenBooks' edge is plain English over a hidden ledger; QBO/Xero leak COA jargon here. If the owner has to understand accounting to onboard, you've lost the small-business persona.
- Auto-posting low-confidence AI classifications to look 'more automated'. Violates 'AI proposes, the ledger posts' and the thresholds (suggest never auto-posts; balanced 0.90; autopilot 0.75). Low-confidence items must route to the Inbox/Review Queue, not the ledger.
- Marketing-style or 'purple AI' onboarding ornament — gradients, emoji, glassmorphism, decorative blobs, alarm-red expenses. Violates the design system; keep it quiet, white ledger surfaces, one brand green, money-in green / expenses neutral.
- Static rules with no visible learning loop. The durable retention hook (Puzzle/Digits) is AI that learns from corrections; if corrections don't visibly reduce future work, the owner won't trust autopilot or build the approval habit.
- Passive feature tours instead of learn-by-doing. 'Next'-button tours underperform interactive walkthroughs that require completing the real action (Attention Insight +10% from the switch); have the owner approve a real proposed category, not watch a demo of it.
- Too many checklist items or items mapped to features not goals. Keep to 3-5 activation-mapped items ('Connect your bank', not 'Explore the integrations tab'), include an explore-on-my-own exit, and use endowed progress (pre-check the first).

## How to let a non-technical small-business owner self-host OpenBooks (Next.js on Vercel + Convex backend + their own GitHub) via a "setup agent skill" — with the unavoidable manual steps (Plaid redirect URI registration, Stripe webhook endpoint creation) made as close to one-click as the third parties allow. The hard constraint is that Plaid and Stripe both have config that *cannot* be set without an account the user owns, so the right design is a hybrid: the agent provisions everything it can via API/Deploy-Button, then hands the user a short, copy-paste, URL-prefilled checklist for the 2-3 things only they can do. Surfacing the exact redirect URI and webhook URL (derived from the user's own deployment domain) is the make-or-break UX detail.

**Principles**

- Split the world into 'agent can do it' (clone repo, create projects, set non-secret env, generate own deploy keys, register Stripe webhook via API key the user pastes once) vs 'only the human can do it' (authorize OAuth/GitHub/Vercel, paste secret keys, click 'Add redirect URI' / 'verify webhook' in a third-party dashboard). Design the skill around that line, not around 'fully automatic'.
- The make-or-break detail of self-hosting a Plaid+Stripe app is surfacing the two URLs that depend on the user's *own* final domain: the Plaid Allowed Redirect URI (e.g. https://their-app.vercel.app/plaid-oauth) and the Stripe webhook endpoint URL (their Convex HTTP action URL, e.g. https://<deployment>.convex.site/stripe/webhook). These cannot be known until after deploy, so the setup flow must be: deploy first, then read back the real domain, then display/auto-register these URLs.
- Use the Vercel Deploy Button as the front door for non-technical users: a single URL (vercel.com/new/clone?repository-url=...) clones the repo into the user's GitHub, creates the Vercel project, and renders a form for required env vars — no terminal required. Pair `env` (the required keys) with `envDescription` and `envLink` pointing at your own setup docs so each blank explains itself.
- NEVER prefill secrets via Deploy Button. Vercel explicitly warns the deploy URL is saved in browser history, so `envDefaults` is only safe for non-sensitive config (public URLs, feature flags). API keys, Plaid/Stripe secrets, and Convex deploy keys must always be typed by the user into the form, never embedded in the link.
- Stripe is the most automatable of the three: a webhook endpoint can be created entirely via API (POST /v1/webhook_endpoints with url + enabled_events), and the signing secret comes back in the response `secret` field — but ONLY on creation. So the setup agent should create the webhook for the user once they paste a restricted Stripe secret key, capture whsec_... immediately, and write it straight into Convex env — the user never has to hunt for it in the dashboard.
- Plaid is the least automatable: Allowed Redirect URIs can only be added in the Plaid Dashboard (Team Settings → API → Allowed redirect URIs → Configure → Add New URI). There is no public Plaid API to register them. So the skill's correct move is to (a) compute the exact HTTPS redirect URI from the user's deployed domain, (b) display it with a one-click copy button, and (c) deep-link the user to the exact Plaid dashboard page, then wait for them to confirm. Treat it as a guided manual step, not an automated one.
- Provide a 'bring-your-own-key' setup wizard inside the running app (post-deploy) rather than cramming everything into the deploy form. Industry pattern (Appwrite, FusionAuth, Supabase self-host, Hermes) is: deploy with the bare minimum to boot, then a first-run wizard collects provider keys, validates each with a test call, and shows green/red status — far less intimidating than a 12-field deploy form.
- Validate every credential the moment it's entered. A good setup agent does not just store a Plaid/Stripe key — it makes a cheap test call (e.g. Plaid /institutions/get in sandbox, Stripe GET /v1/account) and reports success inline, so a non-technical user knows immediately whether they pasted the right value, instead of discovering a broken integration days later.
- Order matters: deploy → read real domain → set domain-derived URLs (Plaid redirect, Stripe webhook) → register them → then connect a bank. Encode this as an ordered, resumable checklist with per-step status, because non-technical self-hosters will abandon if a step fails silently or if they're asked for a URL that doesn't exist yet.

**Apply to OpenBooks**

- Ship a 'Deploy your own OpenBooks' button in the README and on the landing page: a Vercel Deploy Button URL of the form https://vercel.com/new/clone?repository-url=<openbooks-repo>&project-name=openbooks&repository-name=openbooks&env=CONVEX_DEPLOY_KEY,NEXT_PUBLIC_CONVEX_URL,PLAID_CLIENT_ID,PLAID_SECRET,STRIPE_SECRET_KEY&envDescription=Paste%20the%20keys%20from%20your%20setup%20checklist&envLink=https%3A%2F%2Fopenbooks.app%2Fself-host. Use `env` for required keys, `envDescription` + `envLink` to explain them, and DO NOT put any secret in `envDefaults`.
- Build a first-run 'Setup' wizard route in the app (post-deploy) that walks: 1) Convex connection check, 2) Plaid sandbox keys + redirect URI registration, 3) Stripe test keys + webhook registration, 4) connect first bank. Each step shows a live validation badge (calls the provider's API to confirm the key works) and can be re-run.
- Compute and display the Plaid redirect URI from the live deployment domain: <app-origin>/plaid-oauth (the page that handles OAuth return). Show it in a read-only field with a Copy button, plus a 'Open Plaid Dashboard → Allowed redirect URIs' deep link to https://dashboard.plaid.com/developers/api (Team Settings → API → Allowed redirect URIs → Configure). Add a 'I've added it' confirmation that re-checks the OAuth flow.
- Automate the Stripe webhook end-to-end inside the wizard: ask the user to paste a Stripe secret key (test mode), then have a Convex action call POST https://api.stripe.com/v1/webhook_endpoints with url set to the OpenBooks Convex HTTP-action URL (https://<deployment>.convex.site/stripe/webhook) and enabled_events for the events OpenBooks consumes (e.g. payment_intent.succeeded, charge.succeeded, payout.paid, checkout.session.completed). Capture the returned `secret` (whsec_...) and write it directly to Convex env as STRIPE_WEBHOOK_SECRET — never ask the user to copy it manually, because it's only returned once.
- Surface the Stripe webhook URL even for users who'd rather click in the dashboard: display the Convex .convex.site webhook URL with a Copy button and a deep link to https://dashboard.stripe.com/test/webhooks, listing the exact events to enable, so both 'automated' and 'manual' paths land at the same correct URL.
- For Convex provisioning, document two tiers: (a) the simplest path — user runs `npx convex dev` / `npx convex deploy` once with a CONVEX_DEPLOY_KEY they generate in the Convex dashboard, pasted into Vercel env; (b) an advanced 'setup agent' path that uses Convex Platform/Management APIs (base https://api.convex.dev/v1, Bearer team access token from dashboard → Team Settings → Access Tokens, or an OAuth app for managing other users' projects) to create the project and deploy key programmatically. Make clear tier (a) is the supported non-technical path today.
- Write a SELF_HOST.md / setup-agent skill manifest that hard-codes the agent-vs-human split: agent does (clone via button, set non-secret env, create Stripe webhook via API, write back whsec_ and domain-derived URLs into Convex env); human does (authorize GitHub+Vercel+Convex login, paste Plaid/Stripe/Convex secret keys, click 'Add redirect URI' in Plaid, confirm). Each human step gets a prefilled URL + copy-able value + a 'done?' re-check.
- Gate the app's bank-connect and payments features behind setup completion: if PLAID_REDIRECT_URI isn't allowlisted or STRIPE_WEBHOOK_SECRET is missing, show an inline 'Finish setup' banner linking back to the wizard step, rather than letting the user hit a cryptic OAuth or signature-verification failure.
- Because OpenBooks is sandbox/test-only by contract, default the wizard to Plaid Sandbox and Stripe test mode, and note that Sandbox uniquely allows http://localhost redirect URIs — useful for users self-hosting locally before they have a domain. Make the wizard detect localhost vs deployed and show the right redirect URI accordingly.

**Pitfalls**

- Trying to fully automate Plaid redirect-URI registration — there is no public Plaid API for Allowed Redirect URIs; it's dashboard-only. Designing the skill to 'just do it' will fail; the only correct pattern is compute-the-URL + copy button + deep link + manual confirm.
- Embedding any secret in the Deploy Button URL (via envDefaults or query string). Vercel warns the URL is saved in browser history; Plaid/Stripe secret keys and Convex deploy keys leaked this way are a real breach. Secrets must always be typed into the form or the in-app wizard.
- Asking the user to manually copy the Stripe webhook signing secret. The `secret`/whsec_ is returned only at creation time via the API; if you don't capture it programmatically in that moment, the user is left digging in the dashboard (where Stripe only shows it behind a 'reveal' click) — a classic non-technical drop-off point.
- Hard-coding or guessing the redirect/webhook URLs before deploy. They depend on the user's final domain (their-app.vercel.app or custom domain, and the per-deployment <deployment>.convex.site host). Generating them too early produces URLs that don't match what Plaid/Stripe will actually call, causing silent OAuth and signature failures.
- Mismatched Stripe webhook URL host: OpenBooks' webhook lives on the Convex HTTP-actions host (.convex.site), NOT the Next.js/Vercel host. A self-hoster who registers the Vercel URL will get a 404 and no events. The wizard must surface the correct .convex.site URL explicitly.
- Treating Convex Platform-API provisioning as the default non-technical path. Team access tokens are owned by the logged-in member and deleting that member deletes their dev deployments; full programmatic project creation needs an OAuth app and isn't a beginner flow. The supported beginner path is a user-generated CONVEX_DEPLOY_KEY pasted into Vercel.
- Forgetting that Stripe test mode and live mode have separate webhook endpoints, keys, and signing secrets. A wizard that registers a test webhook but the user later flips to live keys will appear 'set up' yet receive nothing — the status check must be mode-aware.
- Cramming all configuration into the deploy-time env form. A 10-field form of opaque keys is where non-technical users bounce. Boot with minimum env, then collect provider keys in an in-app wizard that validates each one with a live test call and shows green/red.
- No validation / no test call on entered keys. Storing a key without verifying it (Stripe GET /v1/account, Plaid /institutions/get sandbox) means failures surface much later as broken bank-connect or missing webhooks, which a non-technical user cannot diagnose.

## Public no-login "try it now" demo / sandbox patterns for data-heavy financial SaaS — how to let a stranger experience the full OpenBooks loop (data in -> classify -> post -> Inbox -> Reports) with zero signup, without exposing real tenants, without abuse risk, and with a clean path to convert into a real workspace.

**Principles**

- Zero-friction entry is the conversion lever. The market leader for this exact category (QuickBooks Online 'Test Drive') opens a fully functional sample company at a single URL with NO email, NO password, NO payment method — only a bot/security check (captcha). For a free OSS bookkeeping tool, this is the right bar: one click to a working ledger.
- Writable-but-disposable beats read-only for a financial product. QBO's test drive is fully writable — users create invoices, categorize transactions, run reports — and 'every change resets when you close the browser.' Read-only demos kill the 'AI proposes, you approve' moment that IS the OpenBooks pitch. Let visitors actually approve Inbox items and watch Reports change; just throw the state away.
- Treat the demo as a real tenant, not a code path fork. The safest isolation is a dedicated demo workspace that flows through the exact same workspace-scoped queries/mutations/auth checks as paying tenants (Convex: every function re-checks workspace membership server-side). Never special-case 'if demo, skip auth' — that's how you leak. Isolation = the demo is just another workspaceId the anonymous session is scoped to.
- Reset on a schedule AND give each visitor a fresh clone. Two complementary resets: (a) per-session — each anonymous visitor gets their own throwaway demo workspace cloned from a golden 'template' workspace, so two strangers never see each other's edits; (b) scheduled — a cron periodically deletes expired/orphaned demo workspaces and re-snapshots the template to prevent 'sandbox drift' as the product evolves.
- Synthetic data only — production data must NEVER enter the demo. Every source consulted is emphatic: 'Real, sensitive production data never enters a sandbox environment.' Build the demo company from hand-curated synthetic records (fake customers, vendors, bank lines). This also sidesteps Plaid/Stripe — like QBO, the demo cannot offer live bank connection ('You cannot test bank feed reconciliation'); pre-seed the bank feed instead.
- Compelling demo data is a narrative, not a random dump. The seed should tell a recognizable small-business story: a named demo business, a handful of customers with paid + overdue invoices (so AR aging has something to show), vendors with open bills (AP aging), a month of bank transactions that are mostly auto-classified with a few left deliberately uncertain in the Inbox, one payroll run, and reports that already look 'lived-in.' Statistically realistic dates/amounts (salary on payday, seasonal spend) read as believable.
- Give the demo a one-way door OUT to signup ('claim/keep this'). The demo's job is to end in a signup. Surface a persistent 'Create your free account to keep going' affordance; on signup, either clone the demo workspace's structure into the new real workspace or start them clean — but never silently lose what they built without offering the upgrade. Anonymous-to-registered handoff is the conversion event.
- Budget and rate-limit the demo as shared infrastructure. A public writable endpoint that clones a workspace per visitor is an abuse magnet (clone storms, AI-cost farming, scraping). Per-IP/per-session rate limits on demo creation, a hard cap on concurrent live demo workspaces, AI features capped or stubbed in demo mode, and bot-check before provisioning.

**Apply to OpenBooks**

## Security posture and test strategy for a bring-your-own-key (BYOK), open-source AI bookkeeping app before public launch. Because OpenBooks is open-source, the threat model is unusual: the entire server codebase, auth logic, and ledger invariants are public, so the only secrets are (a) per-tenant BYOK credentials at rest (Plaid/Stripe/AI keys) and (b) webhook signing secrets — defense can rely on no obscurity. Three load-bearing controls dominate: (1) secrets are encrypted at rest and NEVER returned to any client, (2) every inbound webhook (Plaid ES256 JWT, Stripe HMAC v1) is cryptographically verified with a replay window before any ledger write, and (3) every Convex query/mutation/action re-checks workspace/entity authorization server-side (the OWASP API #1 risk, BOLA, is ~40% of API attacks). The test pyramid for a money system inverts the usual emphasis: accounting invariants (debits==credits, immutability, idempotent posting) get exhaustive property-based unit coverage; integration tests cover webhook->classify->post; a thin e2e layer proves the mandatory v1 loop. The deliverable is a documented pre-launch security audit checklist plus a CI gate (gitleaks/trufflehog secret scanning + dependency SCA).

**Principles**

- AI proposes, the ledger posts: security boundary must sit at the ledger mutation, not the AI layer. An attacker who manipulates AI classification should never be able to post an unbalanced or cross-tenant journal entry, because the single posting mutation independently re-validates tenant scope and debits==credits.
- Open-source = no security through obscurity. Auth logic, ledger code, and schema are public, so the ONLY secrets are BYOK credentials and webhook signing secrets. Every control must hold even when the attacker has read the source. This raises the bar on encryption-at-rest and per-tenant authorization rather than lowering it.
- Secrets encrypted at rest + never serialized to the client. Per-tenant API keys (Plaid access_token, Stripe restricted key, AI provider key) are stored encrypted with AES-256-GCM under a server-held key (Convex env var / KMS), decrypted only inside actions at call time, and stripped from every query return shape. Auditors specifically check: if a DB backup leaks, are credentials plaintext? (Snyk: 28M credentials leaked on GitHub in 2025.)
- Webhook authenticity is a hard gate before any side effect. Treat every inbound webhook as untrusted input: verify the signature (Stripe HMAC-SHA256 v1 via constructEvent on the RAW body; Plaid ES256 JWT with body-sha256 integrity check), enforce a <=5-minute replay window, and only then enqueue. No ledger write, no balance change, no email on an unverified payload.
- BOLA defense on EVERY read and write, derived from the caller's identity — never from a client-supplied tenant/workspace ID. OWASP API1:2023 (Broken Object Level Authorization) is the #1 API risk and ~40% of attacks. Each Convex function resolves workspaceId from the authenticated session, then asserts the target entity belongs to that workspace before touching it. Random UUIDs are hardening, not the boundary.
- Idempotency is a correctness invariant, not an optimization, for a money system. Webhook retries, Plaid+Stripe deposit/payout double-reporting, and re-syncs must never double-post. Dedupe keyed on provider event.id (and external txn id) inside the SAME transaction as the ledger write, so a crash between 'recorded' and 'posted' cannot double-fulfill.
- Inverted test pyramid for financial correctness: the widest, cheapest layer is property-based unit tests over ledger invariants (every posting balances; posted entries are immutable; reversal+repost is the only correction; SUM of all account balances net to the accounting identity). Integration tests cover the webhook->match/rule/AI->post pipeline; a thin e2e layer proves the full v1 loop end to end.
- Shift-left secret + dependency scanning as a launch gate, not a one-time scan. Gitleaks/detect-secrets as a pre-commit hook, TruffleHog (with live verification) in CI on full history, plus SCA (npm audit / Trivy / Dependabot) and an SBOM. A public repo means a single committed live key is instantly harvestable — the constant-time rule is that no live Plaid/Stripe key ever enters any env store (sandbox/test only).
- Fail closed and minimize blast radius: subscribe to only the webhook event types you handle, return 2xx fast then process async, log auth denials and verification failures for monitoring, and scope every external credential to least privilege (Stripe restricted keys, Plaid product scoping).

**Apply to OpenBooks**

- Webhook verification gate (Stripe): in the Convex HTTP action for the Stripe route, read the RAW request body (do not let any JSON middleware re-serialize it), call stripe.webhooks.constructEvent(rawBody, sigHeader, signingSecret), reject with 400 on SignatureVerificationError, accept only v1 scheme signatures, and enforce the default 5-minute timestamp tolerance. Add a unit test that a tampered body and an expired timestamp both reject.
- Webhook verification gate (Plaid): implement the full ES256 JWT verification before processing any Plaid webhook — read Plaid-Verification header, decode JWT header unverified, reject if alg != ES256, extract kid, fetch the JWK via /webhook_verification_key/get (cache by kid, invalidate on rotation), verify the ES256 signature, enforce iat within 300s, and constant-time-compare request_body_sha256 against SHA-256 of the raw body (note: whitespace-sensitive, 2-space formatting). Treat any failure as untrusted and drop.
- Webhook idempotency table: add a processedWebhookEvents table keyed on (provider, eventId) with a unique index; in the SAME mutation that posts to the ledger, insert the dedupe row and perform the posting — if the row exists, no-op and return 2xx. This closes Stripe retries AND the known Plaid-deposit->Stripe-payout double-count (memory E7) at the write boundary, not just at match time.
- Secrets-at-rest audit: confirm every BYOK credential (Plaid access_token, Stripe key, AI provider key, Plunk PLUNK_SECRET_KEY, payroll payTo bank details) is stored encrypted (AES-256-GCM) under a server-held key and decrypted only inside actions. Memory flags two open gaps to close before launch: payroll payTo is NOT encrypted at rest, and full bank details are stored — both must be encrypted and admin-only.
- Client serialization scan: write a test/assertion that no Convex query return shape includes a credential field. Grep the schema for token/secret/apiKey/payTo fields and verify each is either excluded from query selectors or redacted (e.g., last-4 only). A query that returns a Plaid access_token to the browser is the BYOK-app failure mode.
- Per-tenant authorization helper applied to EVERY function: introduce a single requireWorkspaceMember(ctx) + assertEntityInWorkspace(ctx, doc, workspaceId) helper, resolve workspaceId from the authenticated session (never from args), and call it at the top of every query/mutation/action that touches workspace-scoped data. Add negative tests: user A requesting user B's invoice/transaction/journal-entry id returns not-found/forbidden, not the object.
- Ledger invariant unit suite (property-based): for every generated posting, assert sum(debits)==sum(credits); assert posted journal entries are immutable (mutation to post one throws); assert the only correction path is reverse+repost; assert money is integer minor units + currency code (no floats); assert the single posting mutation rejects any unbalanced batch. Run as the widest, fastest layer.
- Integration tests for the v1 pipeline: feed a verified Plaid/Stripe/CSV item through match->rule->memory->AI->post and assert confident items post once and uncertain items land in the Inbox; assert the autonomy thresholds (suggest=never auto-post, balanced=0.90, autopilot=0.75) are read from the single shared constant and gate auto-posting correctly.
- CI security gate: add gitleaks (or detect-secrets) as a pre-commit hook and a CI job, TruffleHog full-history scan with verification, npm/pnpm audit (or Trivy) for dependency CVEs, and an SBOM artifact. Add an explicit CI assertion that no live Plaid/Stripe keys are present — only sandbox/test keys (AGENTS.md ban on live keys).
- Reports-from-ledger verification: a test that financial reports query journal lines (not ad hoc category totals) and that a posted+reversed pair nets to zero in every report, proving corrections flow through the immutable ledger.
- Pre-launch security audit checklist as a doc artifact: secrets at rest (AES-256-GCM, key mgmt/rotation), no secrets to client, both webhook verifications + replay window + idempotency, per-tenant authz on every function with negative tests, secret+dependency scanning in CI, least-privilege external keys, HTTPS/TLS1.2+ webhook routes, CSRF-exempt + signature-gated webhook endpoints, audit logging of auth denials, and a rollback/incident note. Publish via artifact-publisher with the AI copy button per the working agreement.

**Pitfalls**

- Verifying the Stripe signature against a JSON-parsed/re-serialized body. Any framework JSON middleware silently rewrites the payload and breaks HMAC verification; the route MUST consume the raw bytes. This is the single most common Stripe webhook security bug.
- Skipping the replay window. A valid (signature, payload) pair re-sent later still verifies cryptographically — without the iat/timestamp <=5min check plus event.id dedupe, an attacker (or an innocent retry) can replay a 'payment succeeded' and double-post.
- Idempotency dedupe NOT in the same transaction as the ledger write. If you mark-processed and then post in two steps, a crash between them either drops the event or double-posts on retry. The dedupe insert and the posting must be one atomic mutation.
- Trusting a client-supplied workspaceId/tenantId. Deriving scope from args instead of the authenticated session is textbook BOLA — the #1 API risk and ~40% of API attacks. Random UUIDs feel safe but are NOT the boundary; only a server-side ownership check is.
- Returning credentials to the client. A query that includes a Plaid access_token, Stripe key, or payroll bank detail in its return shape leaks it to the browser/network tab even if 'the UI doesn't show it.' Open-source makes this trivially discoverable.
- Storing secrets reversibly-but-unencrypted (or in plaintext). Known OpenBooks gaps: payroll payTo bank details are not encrypted at rest. A leaked DB backup then exposes real banking data — the exact scenario auditors probe.
- A single committed live key in a public repo. Because the repo is open-source, a live Plaid/Stripe key pushed even briefly is harvested within minutes by automated scanners. The AGENTS.md sandbox/test-only rule must be CI-enforced, not just documented.
- Subscribing to all webhook event types and assuming ordered delivery. Handling events you don't need widens the attack surface; assuming order (e.g., invoice.paid before invoice.created) causes incorrect ledger state. Process each event independently and re-fetch from the API for sensitive operations.
- Letting AI confidence bypass the ledger's own checks. If the posting mutation trusts the AI/classification layer's tenant or balance, a manipulated suggestion can cross tenants or post unbalanced. The mutation must independently re-assert scope and debits==credits regardless of how confident the proposer was.
- A top-heavy test pyramid for a money app. Relying on slow e2e tests while under-testing ledger invariants means double-entry violations and idempotency bugs slip through; the property-based invariant layer must be the widest, and it must run on every commit.
- Forgetting CSRF-exemption + auth on the webhook route done wrong: the route must be CSRF-exempt (it can't carry a CSRF token) but is then ONLY protected by signature verification — if you also accidentally drop signature verification while debugging, the endpoint becomes fully open to forged ledger writes.

## Owners don't need accounting training; they need just enough of a mental model to TRUST that an automated ledger is correct and to know when to intervene. The winning approach is a "hidden ledger, plain-English surface" with an embedded, just-in-time education layer plus three trust-building UX pillars: (1) calibrated confidence on every AI decision, (2) full provenance/drill-down on every number ("explain this number" → traces to source transactions and journal lines), and (3) an immutable audit log that proves nothing was silently changed. Teach the minimal four concepts only at the moment they become load-bearing — double-entry as a self-checking system, categories=accounts, cash vs accrual, and reconciliation/"closed books" — rather than front-loading an accounting course. Google PAIR's explainability guidance is the strongest design backbone: partial explanations by default, tie explanations to user actions, show N-best alternatives instead of raw percentages, and progressively expand automation as trust accrues.

**Principles**

- AI proposes, the ledger posts — and SAY SO in the UI. PAIR's three trust foundations (ability, reliability, benevolence) map directly: prove competence with a self-checking ledger, prove reliability with consistent posting, prove benevolence by being honest that the system catches its own mistakes.
- Teach just-in-time, not up-front. Owners learn the minimal concept only when it becomes load-bearing (first reconciliation, first accrual question, first month-end). Front-loading a bookkeeping course kills adoption; PAIR recommends explanations 'in response to a user's action' to create clear cause-and-effect.
- The minimal conceptual model is exactly four ideas, taught in plain English: (1) double-entry = a self-checking system where every dollar has a source and a destination so the books catch their own errors (Assets = Liabilities + Equity must always balance); (2) your plain-English 'categories' ARE accounts under the hood; (3) cash vs accrual = 'count it when money moves' vs 'count it when the work happens'; (4) reconciliation = 'does our record match the bank's record', and 'closed books' = 'this month is finalized and locked'.
- Hide debits/credits from owners entirely. Owners should never see the words 'debit' and 'credit'. They see money in (can be green), money out (neutral, never alarm red), and category. The double-entry mechanics stay in the hidden ledger — but remain inspectable on demand for anyone curious or for an accountant.
- Calibrate confidence, don't just display it. PAIR: prefer categorical (High/Med/Low with defined cutoffs) or N-best alternatives ('this looks like Software or Office Supplies') over raw percentages, which non-technical users misread and over-trust. Map confidence cutoffs to the existing autonomy constants: autopilot=0.75, balanced=0.90, suggest=never auto-post.
- Every number must be explainable and traceable to source. 'Explain this number' / drill-down from any report figure to the journal lines to the originating bank/Stripe/invoice transaction is the single highest-trust feature in finance UX — it lets owners self-answer 'where did this come from?' and proves the number isn't a black box.
- Provenance on every posted item: show WHERE it came from (Plaid/Stripe/CSV/manual), HOW it was classified (transfer match / rule / memory / AI / manual), and by WHOM/WHAT. PAIR's three data dimensions — scope, reach, removal — should be visible.
- Make the audit log immutable and visible as a trust artifact, not a compliance afterthought. Record who/what changed, when, and the before/after. Posted entries are immutable; corrections reverse-and-repost rather than edit-in-place — and the UI should explain WHY ('we never silently change a posted number; we reverse it and post a correction so your history is intact').
- Handle AI mistakes gracefully and let the system learn. PAIR: acknowledge the error, explain what happened, offer a correction path, and let the user 'teach the system the correct outcome' (feeds the rule/memory layer). One wrong auto-categorization that's easy to fix builds more trust than one that's hidden.
- Progressive automation: start in 'suggest' (never auto-post), let the owner approve from the Inbox, and earn the right to raise autonomy as the memory/rule layer proves itself. PAIR's trust timeline: communicate limits early → sandbox/preview → gradually increase automation with feedback loops → proactively explain recovery when something goes wrong.
- Reconciliation is the owner's confidence ritual. Frame it as 'we checked our books against your bank and everything matches' (or 'these 3 items need your eyes'). It's the moment that converts an abstract ledger into trusted truth; ~30% of small businesses have record errors precisely because they skip it.
- Help-center structure should mirror the product's mental model, not an accountant's syllabus: a task-based 'Getting Started' track, short how-to articles tied to specific screens, embedded micro-explainers (tooltips/popovers), and tiered depth (beginner → 'for the curious' → 'for your accountant'), matching QuickBooks' beginner/intermediate/advanced and Intuit-for-Education's leveled curricula.

- Auto-posting low-confidence items to feel 'magical'. A confidently-wrong auto-posted entry that's hard to find destroys trust far more than it builds. Route uncertainty to the Inbox; never auto-post below threshold.

- Alarm-red styling on ordinary expenses. The design rules forbid it; treating every expense as a warning trains owners to distrust/ignore the system. Money out should be neutral.
- Provenance/audit log that exists but is buried or unreadable. If owners can't see where a number came from in 1-2 clicks, the trust benefit evaporates. Drill-down must terminate at a human-readable source, not an opaque ID.
- Over-explaining low-stakes items. PAIR: scale explanation depth to stakes. A $4 coffee categorization doesn't need a paragraph; a $40k payroll run or a tax-relevant reclassification does.

- Help center organized by accounting taxonomy instead of by what the owner is trying to do. Task-based IA ('Send your first invoice', 'Check why this number changed') beats topic-based ('Accounts Receivable').

- [https://pair.withgoogle.com/chapter/explainability-trust/](https://pair.withgoogle.com/chapter/explainability-trust/) — Google PAIR: use partial explanations by default, tie explanations to user actions, prefer categorical or N-best confidence over raw percentages (which non-technical users over-trust), use example-based explanations, handle errors by acknowledging + offering correction paths, and progressively increase automation as trust builds across a beginning→early-use→ongoing→error-recovery timeline.
- [https://mondialsoftware.com/the-importance-of-transaction-level-drill-down-in-financial-auditing/](https://mondialsoftware.com/the-importance-of-transaction-level-drill-down-in-financial-auditing/) — Transaction-level drill-down from summary report figures to underlying records ('explain this number') promotes transparency and is crucial for building stakeholder trust; data provenance + auditability form the backbone of trustworthy accounting systems.
- [https://quickbooks.intuit.com/learn-support/en-us/](https://quickbooks.intuit.com/learn-support/en-us/) — QuickBooks structures owner education as beginner/intermediate/advanced tracks with a Help Center, Live Experts, and Community Q&A, plus leveled Intuit-for-Education curricula — a model for tiered, task-based help-center IA.
- [https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/quantumblack/our-insights/building-ai-trust-the-key-role-of-explainability](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/quantumblack/our-insights/building-ai-trust-the-key-role-of-explainability) — McKinsey: 40% of respondents identified explainability as a key risk in adopting generative AI, and explainability is central to building user trust in AI systems.
